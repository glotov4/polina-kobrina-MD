## The Problem

Custom orthoses — external devices used to support, align, or correct the body — are essential in rehabilitation medicine, particularly for patients with cerebral palsy, post-oncology conditions, and musculoskeletal disorders. But in Russia, the materials used to fabricate them are largely imported, expensive, and not always accessible to clinics working with underserved populations, including those supported by charitable foundations.

During my professional retraining in physical and rehabilitation medicine, I worked at a clinic collaborating with foundations supporting children with cerebral palsy and post-oncology patients. I saw firsthand how material cost and availability limited what clinicians could offer. Standard imported thermoplastic sheets used for orthosis fabrication cost roughly **8 times more** than what the raw material should cost. The question was straightforward: could I process a more affordable thermoplastic from raw granules into functional orthosis sheets — and would they actually work on patients?

## The Approach

I chose to focus on **polycaprolactone (PCL)** — a biodegradable thermoplastic polyester with a low melting point (~60°C), which makes it moldable in hot water and shapeable by hand without industrial equipment. PCL is already used in some medical and dental applications, but its use for orthosis fabrication in the Russian rehabilitation context was underexplored — and nobody was processing it from raw granules into ready-to-use sheets at this scale.

My process:

1. **Literature review.** Surveyed existing research on thermoplastic materials in orthotics, with a focus on PCL's mechanical properties, biocompatibility, and clinical applications.

2. **Material sourcing.** Identified and sourced raw PCL granules from suppliers in China — navigating international procurement, comparing specifications, and managing small-batch import logistics independently.

3. **Sheet fabrication from granules.** Developed a process to melt PCL granules and press them into flat sheets of consistent thickness suitable for orthosis molding. This was the core technical challenge: finding the right temperature, pressure, and cooling parameters to produce sheets that were structurally sound and clinically usable.

4. **Clinical testing on patients.** The fabricated PCL sheets were heated in hot water, molded directly onto patients' limbs, and tested in real clinical conditions. The orthoses held their shape, provided the required support, and were comfortable for patients.

## The Result

A validated prototype: **functional orthosis sheets fabricated from raw PCL granules** — moldable at low temperature, lightweight, tested on real patients, and **approximately 8x cheaper** than standard imported thermoplastic orthosis materials available in Russia.

The project demonstrated that PCL processed from raw granules is a viable, clinically tested alternative for orthosis fabrication in resource-limited rehabilitation settings. The sheets can be shaped chairside with minimal equipment (hot water, basic tools), making them especially relevant for:

- Clinics working with charitable foundations (children with CP, post-oncology rehab)
- Settings where imported materials are cost-prohibitive
- Situations requiring rapid prototyping or frequent orthosis adjustments (e.g., growing children)

**Current status:** The project is paused due to customs and material import complications. The technical proof of concept is complete, and the clinical results were positive.

## What I Learned

This project was not part of any formal curriculum or funded research — it was self-initiated and self-managed. It taught me:

- **How to take a clinical observation to a clinically validated product.** From "this material is too expensive" to fabricating sheets from raw granules and fitting them on real patients.
- **How to navigate international sourcing and customs.** Finding reliable suppliers, comparing material specs across languages, managing import logistics — and learning the hard way what can go wrong with customs.
- **How to work at the intersection of medicine and materials engineering.** Understanding material science enough to develop a fabrication process, and understanding rehabilitation enough to define what "clinically functional" actually means.
- **How to run an independent project end-to-end.** No supervisor, no lab, no budget line. Just a problem, a hypothesis, raw material, and the discipline to see it through to patient testing.

### R&D-проект: Доступные ортезные листы из поликапролактона

Во время переподготовки по реабилитационной медицине я работала в клинике, сотрудничающей с фондами помощи детям с ДЦП и пост-онкологическим пациентам. Одна из проблем, которую я видела каждый день: термопластические материалы для индивидуальных ортезов — импортные, дорогие и часто недоступные. Стандартные импортные листы стоили примерно **в 8 раз дороже**, чем должен стоить сам материал.

Я решила проверить: можно ли самостоятельно изготовить ортезные листы из сырья?

Что я сделала:

- Обзор литературы по применению **поликапролактона (PCL)** в ортопедии и реабилитации
- Поиск и закупка **сырых гранул PCL** у поставщиков в Китае
- Разработка процесса **плавки гранул и формовки в листы** нужной толщины
- **Клинические испытания на пациентах** — ортезы из моих листов держали форму, обеспечивали поддержку и были удобны

Результат: функциональные ортезные листы из поликапролактона, изготовленные из сырых гранул, **~8x дешевле импортных аналогов**, протестированные на реальных пациентах с положительным результатом.

Проект приостановлен из-за сложностей с таможней и импортом материала. Но техническое и клиническое подтверждение концепции — получено.

Проект был полностью самостоятельным: без финансирования, без лаборатории, без научного руководителя. От клинической проблемы — через исследование, закупку, производство — до пациента.
